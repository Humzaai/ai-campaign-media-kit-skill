# Copywriting Rules

Write like a premium strategist talking to a busy executive — not like a landing-page generator.

## Voice

- Confident, specific, calm. No exclamation points doing the work of evidence.
- Outcomes first: lead with what the buyer gets, then how.
- Plain words. If a simpler word exists, use it.

## Structure

- Paragraphs of 1–3 sentences.
- Headlines carry the argument — a skimmer reading only headlines should get the full story.
- One idea per section. One section per idea.
- Bullets for scannable lists; prose for reasoning. Never a wall of either.

## Claims

- Every claim is proof-led: number + context + source + date, or it's reframed as an honest description.
- Never write around missing proof with vague intensifiers ("massive reach", "incredible results"). State what is true or mark it pending.
- Specific beats superlative: "took a dev-tool launch to the top of its category's newsletter charts" beats "explosive growth."

## Banned patterns

- Fake urgency ("only 2 slots left!") — unless capacity limits are genuinely real and current
- Guarantee language ("guaranteed ROI", "can't fail")
- Vague hype ("game-changing", "revolutionary", "10x your brand")
- Generic agency filler ("we craft bespoke solutions", "end-to-end synergy")
- Keyword stuffing and awkward SEO phrasing
- CTAs that hide the action: "Click Here", "Submit", "Learn More", "Contact Me"

## Preferred CTA shapes

Action + object, buyer-framed: "Start a Campaign", "Request a Campaign Plan", "View Campaign Results", "Work With Us", "See the Audience".

## Objection handling

Name the objection plainly in FAQ questions ("What if the campaign doesn't perform?") and answer honestly — including what happens when things don't work. Honesty about failure modes is a conversion asset, not a liability.

## Editing pass

Before a page ships: cut 20% of the words, check every headline reads as a claim (not a label), verify no sentence exists that a skeptical CMO would smirk at.
