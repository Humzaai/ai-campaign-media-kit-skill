# Conversion Principles

The reasoning behind every layout and copy decision. These override generic "best practices."

## The five jobs

Every section must do at least one of:

1. **Increase desire** — make the outcome vivid and relevant to the buyer
2. **Increase trust** — show verified proof, sources, public profiles
3. **Reduce risk** — honest stop rules, clear process, no lock-in surprises
4. **Explain the mechanism** — show *why* it works, not just that it does
5. **Make the next step easier** — checklists, prefilled emails, clear expectations

A section that does none of these is deleted. "It looks nice" is not a job.

## The ninety-second buyer

Assume a founder or CMO opening the site from a phone between meetings. They decide in about ninety seconds whether to send an email. Consequences:

- The hero must carry the whole argument alone.
- Headlines must be skimmable as a complete story — copy under them is for the second pass.
- The strongest proof appears early on every page, not saved for a finale.

## Proof beats persuasion

A verified number with a source and date outsells three paragraphs of adjectives. Order of preference: verified metric → verifiable public link → screenshot through the quality gate → honest description → nothing. "Nothing" is always available; fabrication never is.

## Objections are content

The FAQ is not filler — it is where buying decisions actually stall. Handle the real ones: price opacity, timeline, creative control, measurement, "what if it doesn't work." Answering "what if it doesn't work" honestly (stop rules) converts better than dodging it.

## Qualify honestly

"Who this is for / not for" sections cost a few bad-fit leads and earn trust from good-fit ones. A media-kit site that seems willing to take anyone reads as desperate.

## One primary action

Every page ends at the same place: Work With Us or a prefilled email. Multiple competing CTAs split intent. Secondary links move people *along the funnel*, never sideways out of it.

## Email-first, low-friction

An email with a prefilled subject and a what-to-include checklist beats a long form. The buyer stays in their own tool, and the founder gets a qualified first message.
